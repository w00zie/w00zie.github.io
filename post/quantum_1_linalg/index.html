Quantum Information - Part 1: Linear Algebra

Apr 23, 2019 4 min read

In this first post we'll give a brief introduction to the necessary linear algebra for Quantum Mechanics.

Linear Algebra

Vectors

The basic objects of linear algebra are vector spaces. The vector space of most interest in QM is $\mathbb{C}^n$, the space of all $n$-tuples of complex numbers $(z_1, \dots, z_n)$. The elements of a vector space are called vectors. The standard quantum mechanical notation for a vector in a vector space is the following:

\[ \lvert \psi \rangle \]

A spanning set for a vector space is a set of vectors \(\lvert v_1 \rangle, \dots, \lvert v_n \rangle\) such that any vector \(\lvert v \rangle\) can be written as a linear combination \(\lvert v \rangle = \sum_{i} a_i \lvert v_i \rangle\) of vectors in that set. For example, a spanning set for the vector space $\mathbb{C}^2$ is the set:

$$ \lvert v_1 \rangle =

\begin{bmatrix}
1  \\
0 \\
\end{bmatrix}; \quad 
\lvert v_2 \rangle = 
\begin{bmatrix}
0 \\
1 \\
\end{bmatrix}, $$

since any vector

$$ \lvert v \rangle =

\begin{bmatrix}
a_1 \\
a_2 \\
\end{bmatrix} $$

can be expressed as a linear combination $\lvert v \rangle = a_1 \lvert v_1 \rangle + a_2 \lvert v_2 \rangle$ of the vectors $\lvert v_1 \rangle$ and $\lvert v_2 \rangle$.

Generally, a vector space may have many different spanning sets. A second spanning set for $\mathbb{C}^2$ is the set:

$$ \lvert v_1 \rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt2}

\begin{bmatrix}
1 \\
1 \\
\end{bmatrix}; \quad 
\lvert v_2 \rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}
\begin{bmatrix}
1 \\
-1 \\
\end{bmatrix}, $$

A set of non-zero vectors $\lvert v_1 \rangle, \dots, \lvert v_n \rangle$ are linearly dependent if there exists a set of complex numbers $a_1, \dots, a_n$ with $a_i \neq 0$ for at least one value of $i$, such that:

\[ a_1 \lvert v_1 \rangle + a_2 \lvert v_2 \rangle + \dots a_n \lvert v_n \rangle = 0 \]

A set of vectors is linearly independent if it is not linearly dependent. A set of linearly independent vectors form a basis for their relative vector space.

Linear operators and matrices

A linear operator between vector spaces $V$ and $W$ is defined to be any function $A:V \to W$ which is linear in its inputs:

\[ A \bigg ( \sum_{i} a_i \lvert v_i \rangle \bigg ) = \sum_{i} a_i A(\lvert v_i \rangle)\]

And we just write $A \lvert v \rangle$ to denote this application.

The most convenient way to understand linear operators is in terms of their matrix representations. In fact, the linear operator and matrix viewpoints turn out to be completely equivalent.

Suppose $\lvert v_1 \rangle, \dots, \lvert v_m \rangle$ is a basis for \(V\) and \(\lvert w_1 \rangle, \dots, \lvert w_n \rangle\) is a basis for \(W\). Then $\forall j = {1, \dots, m }$ there exists complex numbers $A_{1j}, \dots, A_{nj}$ such that:

\[ A \lvert v_j \rangle = \sum_{i} A_{ij} \lvert w_i \rangle \]

Inner products

An inner product is a function which takes as input two vectors $\lvert v \rangle$ and $\lvert w \rangle$ from a vector space and produces a complex number as output. The standard notation for the inner product is $\langle v \lvert w \rangle$ where $\langle v \rvert$ is the dual vector to the vector $\lvert v \rangle$. A function $(\cdot , \cdot): V \times V \to \mathbb{C}$ is an inner product if it satisfies:

  1. Linearity in the second argument: \( \bigg (\lvert v \rangle, \sum_{i} \lambda_i \lvert w_i \rangle \bigg ) = \sum_{i} \lambda_{i} (\lvert v \rangle, \lvert w_i \rangle) \)

  2. $(\lvert v \rangle , \lvert w \rangle) = (\lvert w \rangle, \lvert v \rangle)^*$

  3. $(\lvert v \rangle, \lvert v \rangle) \geq 0$ with equality iff $\lvert v \rangle = 0$.

For example $\mathbb{C}^n$ has an inner product defined by:

\[ ((y_1, \dots, y_n ), (z_1, \dots z_n)) = \sum_{i} y^*_{i}z_i \]

We call a vector space with an inner product an inner product space or (usually) an Hilbert space.

We define the norm of a vector $\lvert v \rangle$ by:

\[ || \lvert v \rangle || = \sqrt{\langle v \lvert v \rangle}\]

For any non-zero vector $\lvert v \rangle$ we talk about normalization or normalized form the vector $\lvert v \rangle / || \lvert v \rangle ||$.
